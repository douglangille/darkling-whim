**Assistant:**

In animals, a **“paint” coat pattern** refers to a type of **pinto spotting**—large, irregular patches of white mixed with another base color.  

- **Origin of the term:** It comes mainly from horse coat terminology. A “Paint Horse” is a recognized breed in North America, but the pattern itself (paint/pinto) can appear in many horse breeds, dogs, cats, and even cattle.  
- **Pattern traits:**
  - Large, often asymmetrical white patches across the body.  
  - Base coat (black, bay, chestnut, etc.) shows through in sharp contrast.  
  - Markings can extend onto the face and legs.  
- **Variants:** In horses, the “paint” look can be classified further (tobiano, overo, tovero), depending on how the white patches are arranged.  
- **Use beyond horses:** In dogs (like Australian Shepherds) or cats, people sometimes loosely call certain bicolor or tricolor coats “painted” if the patches are bold and irregular.  

So if you see “paint” as an animal coat pattern, it basically means: **big, bold splashes of color and white, as if painted on.**

**Assistant:**

That pattern is usually called **“spangled” or “mottled”** in poultry genetics, depending on the exact look:  

- **Mottled**: irregular splashes or spots of color on a white base, often looking like paint flecks. The edges of the spots can be soft or ragged.  
- **Spangled**: more organized spots—often teardrop- or crescent-shaped patches of darker color at the end of each feather, giving a flecked or “snowflake” look.  
- **Exchequer / splash variants**: some breeds (like Exchequer Leghorns or “splash” Orpingtons) produce grey or black speckles on a white background, very much like a paint-splatter effect.  

So a chicken that’s mostly white with grey splatters, like brush-dropped paint, would be described as **mottled (grey on white)** or **splash** if it comes from a blue dilution line.

**Assistant:**

Yes — black, grey, and white striping in even, horizontal bands across the whole body is the classic **barred** pattern, and the most common barred breed people keep is the **Barred Plymouth Rock** (often just called “Barred Rock”).  

- **Barred vs. cuckoo**: Barred Rock barring is crisp and even, with regular black/white striping. In “cuckoo” barred breeds (like Marans), the stripes are more blurry and uneven.  
- **Sex-linked trick**: In Barred Rocks, males are usually lighter overall (wider white bars), while females are darker (narrower white bars).  
- **Breed context**: If your bird is chunky-bodied with a calm temperament, lays brown eggs, and has a single comb → almost certainly a Barred Plymouth Rock.

**Assistant:**

“Black chickens” can mean a few different things, depending on how *black* we’re talking:  

### 1. Solid black (shiny green sheen in the sun)  
- **Australorp** – big, friendly, heavy layers of large brown eggs. Australian origin.  
- **Jersey Giant** – even larger, bred as dual-purpose (meat + eggs). Massive frame compared to Australorp.  
- **Orpington (Black)** – rounder, fluffier build, comes in many colors (buff is the most famous).  

### 2. All-black, inside and out (fibromelanistic breeds)  
- **Ayam Cemani** – from Indonesia; jet-black feathers, skin, beak, comb, and even black-tinged meat and bones. Very rare and expensive.  
- **Kadaknath** – Indian fibromelanistic breed, not quite as dramatically black as Cemani but still striking.  

### 3. Utility black hybrids  
- Commercial crosses like **Black Sex-Links** (aka Black Stars) – reliable brown-egg layers, usually black with a bit of red/gold hackle feathering.  

---

**Quick ID tricks**:  
- **Pure glossy black, normal skin** → probably Australorp, Orpington, or Jersey Giant.  
- **Truly black everywhere (skin, tongue, legs)** → Ayam Cemani or Kadaknath.  
- **Black body with red/copper on neck/shoulders** → not “black” per se, but common in sex-link hybrids.
