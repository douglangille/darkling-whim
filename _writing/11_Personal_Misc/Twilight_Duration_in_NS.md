The length of twilight in Nova Scotia depends on the time of year. Twilight occurs in three phases—civil, nautical, and astronomical—each lasting for a different duration.  

- **Civil twilight** (when the sun is 0° to 6° below the horizon) lasts about **30–40 minutes** after sunset or before sunrise.  
- **Nautical twilight** (6° to 12° below the horizon) lasts another **30–35 minutes**.  
- **Astronomical twilight** (12° to 18° below the horizon) adds another **25–30 minutes**.  

So, in total, from sunset to complete darkness (or vice versa at sunrise), twilight can last around **90 to 100 minutes**.  

However, during summer, especially near the solstice in June, twilight lasts longer due to the sun’s shallow angle below the horizon. In some northern parts of Nova Scotia, it may never get completely dark, with a lingering astronomical twilight throughout the short night. Conversely, in winter, twilight is shorter.
