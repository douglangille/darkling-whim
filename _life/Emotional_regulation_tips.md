**Assistant:**

Whether “controlling” emotions is a good idea depends on what you mean by it.  
The SOLVED *Emotions Guide* makes the point that emotions themselves aren’t problems to be suppressed or eliminated—they’re signals. The more sustainable goal isn’t to clamp down on feelings, but to regulate your response to them so they don’t run the show. That means noticing them, naming them, and then choosing an intentional action rather than reacting automatically.  

For ruminative and anxious thought loops specifically, the guide recommends:  

**1. Break the rumination cycle**  
- **Shift attention with intentional action** – Engage in a small, grounding task (make tea, tidy a surface, take a walk). This interrupts the mental loop and gives your brain a new focus.  
- **Physical movement** – Exercise or even light stretching reduces the physiological arousal that fuels repetitive thoughts.  

**2. Use the “Name it to tame it” approach**  
- Label what you’re feeling (“I’m anxious about the meeting” vs. “Everything is falling apart”). This activates more rational brain circuits and reduces emotional intensity.  

**3. Challenge the thought pattern**  
- Ask: *Is this thought useful? Is it fact or prediction? What evidence do I have?*  
- Replace “What if…?” spirals with “Even if… then I can…” statements to regain a sense of agency.  

**4. Build calming habits**  
- Regular mindfulness or breathing practices (e.g., 4-7-8 breathing, box breathing) train your nervous system to return to baseline more quickly.  
- Consistent sleep, movement, and social connection help keep anxiety thresholds lower in general.  

**5. Practice self-compassion instead of self-judgment**  
- Rumination often worsens when you shame yourself for having it. Treat the intrusive thoughts as part of a normal human brain process, not as proof of a flaw. This approach—similar to what the *Procrastination Guide* calls “self-forgiveness”—reduces their grip over time.  

In short: don’t aim to “control” emotions in the sense of bottling them up. Aim to regulate them—notice, name, and respond intentionally—while building habits that make anxiety and rumination less sticky.
