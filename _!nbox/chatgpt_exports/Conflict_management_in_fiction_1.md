## 5 Conflict-Management Styles as Fiction Tools

Based on Thomas and Kilmann‚Äôs model, the five styles are:

- **Competing** ‚Äî High assertiveness, low cooperativeness. Win-at-all-costs. Great for antagonists or single-minded heroes.  
- **Collaborating** ‚Äî High assertiveness, high cooperativeness. Seeks win-win. Ideal for relational depth and co-growth.  
- **Compromising** ‚Äî Moderate on both scales. The ‚Äúmiddle ground‚Äù‚Äîgets something for everyone. Useful for tense, temporary resolutions.  
- **Accommodating** ‚Äî Low assertiveness, high cooperativeness. Sacrifices own needs. Good for caring sidekick types or power-imbalanced dynamics.  
- **Avoiding** ‚Äî Low assertiveness, low cooperativeness. Sidesteps conflict. Use for secretive, emotionally distant, or avoidant characters.

##  Why This Works (from conflict theory)

- The **Thomas‚ÄìKilmann model** is built on two axes‚Äîassertiveness and cooperativeness ÓàÄciteÓàÇturn1search18ÓàÇturn1search19ÓàÅ.
- Each style brings narrative pros/cons:
  - **Competing** = tension, urgency, but risks resentment  
  - **Collaborating** = relational richness, but slower and emotionally heavy  
  - **Compromising** = expedient, but can feel half-baked  
  - **Accommodating** = emotionally generous, but risks character burnout or resentment  
  - **Avoiding** = subtext-rich, but can stall momentum or leave issues unresolved

# ü™ô K.M. Weiland Write-Craft Nuggets

## 1. Story Structure (3-Act Spine)
- **Hook (0‚Äì12%)** ‚Üí Establish character in normal world + hint at Lie.  
- **Inciting Event (~12%)** ‚Üí The Call to change / external disruption.  
- **First Plot Point (25%)** ‚Üí No going back. Character fully engaged.  
- **First Pinch (37%)** ‚Üí Antagonist pressure, reminder of stakes.  
- **Midpoint (50%)** ‚Üí Moment of Truth: new info, reversal, or empowerment.  
- **Second Pinch (62%)** ‚Üí Escalated antagonist threat.  
- **Third Plot Point (75%)** ‚Üí ‚ÄúDark Night of the Soul.‚Äù Apparent defeat/loss.  
- **Climax (88‚Äì99%)** ‚Üí Final confrontation. The Lie tested against the Truth.  
- **Resolution (100%)** ‚Üí Aftermath. New equilibrium.

**Gold Nugget:** Every structural beat is also a **character beat**‚Äînot just plot mechanics.

---

## 2. Character Arc Framework
- **The Lie the Character Believes** ‚Üí Foundational falsehood shaping choices.  
- **The Truth** ‚Üí Counterpoint, gradually revealed/accepted.  
- **The Ghost/Wound** ‚Üí Past event giving rise to the Lie.  
- **Arc Options**:  
  - *Positive Change* ‚Üí From Lie to Truth (classic hero).  
  - *Flat Arc* ‚Üí Holds Truth, changes others.  
  - *Negative Arc* ‚Üí Lie deepens, corrupts, or destroys.

**Gold Nugget:** Conflict is *Lie vs. Truth*, not just *Hero vs. Villain*.  

---

## 3. Conflict Management Styles (applied to characters)
(adapted from Thomas‚ÄìKilmann)
- **Competing** ‚Üí Assertive, uncooperative (win/lose).  
- **Collaborating** ‚Üí Assertive, cooperative (win/win).  
- **Compromising** ‚Üí Middle ground.  
- **Accommodating** ‚Üí Yielding, self-sacrificing.  
- **Avoiding** ‚Üí Evades conflict.  

**Gold Nugget:** Style clashes generate organic conflict. Growth = shifting style.

---

## 4. Scene Structure (micro-beats)
- **Goal** ‚Üí What does character want in this scene?  
- **Conflict** ‚Üí Obstacle that blocks it.  
- **Disaster** ‚Üí Outcome that complicates forward motion.  
- **Sequel (reaction)**: Emotion ‚Üí Thought ‚Üí Decision ‚Üí New Goal.  

**Gold Nugget:** Alternating scene‚Äìsequel rhythm creates propulsion without burnout.

---

## 5. Thematic Integration
- **Theme = Truth vs Lie**.  
- Every major beat should ‚Äútest‚Äù the Lie against the Truth.  
- Antagonist often *embodies the Lie*; Mentor/friend often *models the Truth*.  

**Gold Nugget:** Theme is not preached; it *emerges* through conflict.
