Very messy — but messiness is the point, not the flaw.  
The trick is keeping **causal legibility** while letting **moral and emotional coherence** fray.  Think *The Leftovers*, *Station Eleven*, or *Solaris*: readers stay when they feel lost **with purpose**.

Here’s the tolerance envelope:

---

### 1. **Structural Mess (what you can blur)**
- **Chronology:** nonlinear is fine if every time-jump changes *understanding*, not just order.  
- **Perspective:** multiple narrators can contradict each other as long as each contradiction reveals character, not just cleverness.  
- **World logic:** let civic systems and theology remain ambiguous; explain *how people live inside confusion*, not how the system works.

→ Readers forgive fog when **emotional vectors** are clear: each scene moves toward fear, hope, or surrender.

---

### 2. **Moral Mess (where it earns its keep)**
- None of the three heroes must be “right.” Their actions should save something precious while destroying something else equally human.  
- Don’t resolve the dialectic; **freeze it mid-swing.**  
- Replace victory beats with *recognition beats*—moments where characters see the cost of being right.

---

### 3. **Narrative Spine (the non-negotiable clarity)**
- A single, trackable **object or phenomenon** the reader can follow through the chaos—a data fragment, a melody, a living machine.  
  It’s the story’s heartbeat when everything else disintegrates.
- Keep a clear **emotional timeline** even if the plot fractures: despair → awe → choice → quiet.

---

### 4. **Texture of Mess**
- Dialogues that feel like half-finished therapy sessions.  
- Bureaucratic language beside mystical revelation.  
- Abrupt tonal shifts—from sacred to sardonic to tragic.  
- The world isn’t explained; it’s *experienced through maintenance and mourning.*

---

### 5. **Reader Contract**
Promise no grand reveal, only intimacy amid entropy.  
They’ll read if they believe the collapse matters—not because it’s fixable, but because it’s **recognizably human**.
