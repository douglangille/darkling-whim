ICE detentions—including those involving Canadians—**pre-date Trump and continue under both Democratic and Republican administrations.** The agency’s structure and statutory authority come from the 2002 Homeland Security Act and post-9/11 immigration enforcement policies.  

### What changed under Trump (2017–2021)  
- **Policy:** “Zero Tolerance” and broader use of administrative detention; fewer humanitarian/parole releases.  
- **Practice:** More discretionary detentions for overstays and minor paperwork issues; emphasis on optics of enforcement.  
- **Tone:** Anti-immigrant rhetoric that emboldened front-line agents and state collaborations.  
- **Numbers:** ICE daily population spiked above 50 000 in 2019, compared to ~34 000 in 2016.

### What changed after Trump  
- **Biden (2021–present):** Tried to narrow priorities to “public safety and border security,” but litigation and state resistance limited policy control.  
- **Operational inertia:** Facilities, private-prison contracts, and data systems remained; detentions rose again by 2024–2025 as overall border crossings surged.  
- **Result:** 2025’s record ICE population (~60 000+) isn’t driven by political intent alone but by *volume and bottleneck*—a system never fully re-scaled after Trump’s expansion.

### In short  
ICE detention rates are a **structural phenomenon amplified by political posture**: Trump’s era widened the funnel and normalized aggressive enforcement; subsequent years merely adjusted flow and rhetoric without dismantling capacity.
