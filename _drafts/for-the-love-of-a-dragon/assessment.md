# Assessment: For the Love of a Dragon

**Original Publication**: March 6, 2016 (douglangille.com)  
**Current Word Count**: ~1,200 words  
**Assessment Date**: December 29, 2025

---

## Phase 1: INITIAL ANALYSIS

### Multiple Read Analysis

**First Read (Reader Experience)**:
- Strong hook with the wedding interruption
- Immediately invested in Rodney's dilemma
- The reveal of Maud as dragon is well-executed
- Ending feels deliberately open but satisfying
- Emotional resonance is surprisingly strong for the length
- Larry's death is shocking but feels almost disposable

**Second Read (Story Beats)**:
- Opening: Wedding ceremony, expectation of happily-ever-after
- Inciting incident: Maud crashes, kills Larry
- Rising conflict: Revelation of past relationship
- Escalation: Maud threatens the congregation
- Crisis: Ultimatum delivered
- Climax/Resolution: Suspended at moment of choice

**Third Read (Craft Elements)**:
- First-person POV keeps us in Rodney's conflicted head
- Dialogue carries heavy exposition but feels natural
- Sensory details (sulphur, lavender) ground the fantasy
- Present tense creates urgency
- The open ending is a deliberate structural choice

### Core Elements

**Premise**: A knight must choose between his destined marriage to a princess and his cursed dragon-transformed true love who crashes his wedding.

**Promise**: The title promises a story about loving a dragon - an impossible, transgressive love. The opening promises a disrupted wedding and a choice.

**Payoff**: Yes - the story delivers both the disruption and forces Rodney to confront his true feelings. However, the ending's artificiality (telegraphing his choice while withholding announcement) serves dramatic flourish more than character truth.

**Heart**: The violence of conformity. What we sacrifice when we perform the roles society expects rather than embrace authentic connection.

**Theme**: Love versus duty; acceptance versus rejection; transformation and whether love survives it; the courage to choose an unconventional path. **The wedding itself is the monster**—a ritualized binding that requires Rodney to kill part of himself.

### Causality & Character Agency

**Character Agency**: **Reactive protagonist (Rodney)** driven by **proactive antagonist/love interest (Maud)** - this dynamic is core to the story's power and must be preserved in any expansion.

**Key Discoveries**: **Revealed through dialogue** - The backstory unfolds through confrontation, not discovery. Rodney knows everything; we discover it alongside the wedding guests.

**Plot Driver**: **Circumstances + Past Choices** - Maud forces Rodney to face consequences of his earlier cowardice.

**Expansion Considerations**: 
- **Preserve Rodney's reactive nature in the wedding scene** - His passivity IS the point. He's been avoiding this choice.
- **Maud's agency drives the confrontation** - She forces his hand, which is her power
- **The suspended choice** - While the ending feels artificially delayed, the ambiguity serves reader engagement
- **Rodney's emotional cowardice** - This is the real story, not dragon-slaying heroics

### What Works

1. **Thematic subversion** - The dragon isn't the monster; the wedding/conformity is
2. **Maud's characterization** - Emotional anchor of the story; transformation from love to grief to rage while retaining humanity (lavender scent, tears hissing on scales)
3. **Structure** - Opens in medias res, unfolds backstory through dialogue not exposition
4. **Pacing** - Three clear emotional beats: revelation → confrontation → ultimatum
5. **Sensory grounding** - Sulphur, blood dripping, candles snuffed by wind through broken glass
6. **Dialogue subtext** - "Tangled webs we weave," "I've been called worse"
7. **Rodney's hollow admission** - "I'd rather let the Kingdom burn" reveals he knows his desire but lacks courage
8. **The ending as reader participation** - Forces engagement by stopping at the moment of decision

### What Needs Work

1. **Opening line jarring** - "She's a smoke show" is too modern/flippant for the tone that follows
2. **Larry's death unmotivated** - Clunky exposition ("My best man and best friend Larry, Sir Lawrence to most") during action; feels like shock value
3. **Gwen is passive** - Faints, revives, cries; no agency, just a prop watching her fiancé declare love for someone else
4. **Glamoured congregation does nothing** - Mentioned but has no payoff; should either break free dramatically or be cut
5. **Repetitive accusations** - Maud accuses Rodney of betrayal three separate times (tricked, lied, abandoned); needs consolidation
6. **Dragon's Crystal feels mechanical** - Placeholder explanation; either make it significant to present tension or leave transformation mysterious
7. **Final choice artificially delayed** - Story telegraphs Rodney's decision so clearly that withholding it serves flourish over character truth
8. **Explanatory dialogue restates emotion** - 200-300 words could be cut by trusting subtext over stating what's already shown
9. **Final line announces instead of shows** - "I've made my decision" should be replaced with action that reveals the choice

### Surprises on Re-reading

- **Rodney is a coward, not conflicted** - He chose respectability over authentic connection and knows it
- **Maud is desperate, not threatening** - Her threats are hollow; the stakes are entirely emotional
- **This is Maud's story** - She's the active character fighting for what she wants; Rodney is the prize
- **The pattern suggests early-stage work** - Confident voice and clear theme, but still developing comfort with resolution (2016 date confirms this)

## Phase 2: SCOPE ANALYSIS

### Current Scope

**Word Count**: ~1,200 words  
**Classification**: Short story (lower end)  
**Scene Count**: 1 scene (the wedding disruption)  
**Time Span**: ~10-15 minutes of story time  
**Pacing Assessment**: **Rushed in places, but appropriate for the scope**

The single-scene structure works for the current length, but several elements feel compressed or shortchanged:
- Larry's death is too abrupt to matter
- Gwen has no presence
- The backstory is efficiently delivered but feels summarized rather than lived
- The emotional turning points happen too quickly to fully land

### Expansion Candidates

1. **Maud's transformation scene (flashback)**: Currently told ("I had to watch while you transformed. Mutated. It was awful"). This is THE pivotal moment of the story—witnessing someone you love become something society says is monstrous. This needs to be shown, not told.

2. **Rodney and Maud's original relationship**: We know they were engaged, but we don't feel it. What made their love worth eternity? What made Maud touch the crystal despite his warnings?

3. **The deal/deception**: "We had a deal, Maud" references a post-transformation negotiation where Rodney "rescued" her by sparing her life, then abandoned her. This is character-defining cowardice that deserves its own scene.

4. **Gwen as a person**: She's completely blank. If Rodney is choosing between two loves, we need to understand what Gwen offers beyond "convention and respectability."

5. **Larry's significance**: If his death is supposed to matter, we need to know him first. Otherwise, cut him entirely or make the death more impactful.

6. **The consequences of choice**: What happens next? Either path (dragon or princess) has massive implications. The story could continue past the decision to show the first steps of whichever path he chooses.

7. **Maud's solitary centuries**: "Cursed to live for eons, alone and feared" is one line. Her loneliness and desperation could be developed through flashback or dialogue.

8. **The glamoured congregation breaking free**: Currently mentioned but inert. Could be a powerful visual/symbolic moment of the spell of normalcy shattering.

9. **King Thomas's reaction**: He just witnessed his future son-in-law admit he'd rather burn the kingdom than lose the dragon. That's... significant.

10. **Rodney's internal conflict**: We're told he loves both women, but we don't feel him torn. His internal voice could be richer, more conflicted, more self-aware of his cowardice.

### Boundary Testing

**If 2x longer (~2,400 words)**:
- Add Larry as an actual character in the opening (give him a meaningful moment before death)
- Expand Maud's emotional journey in the present scene
- Give Gwen agency—a line of dialogue, a choice of her own
- Deepen Rodney's internal conflict with richer interiority
- Show the glamour breaking
- Add a brief flashback to the transformation moment
- Continue past the decision to show initial consequences

**If 4x longer (~5,000 words - novelette territory)**:
- Structure would need to change fundamentally
- Multiple scenes: The wedding, flashback to transformation, flashback to the deal, return to present, aftermath
- Develop all three characters fully (Rodney, Maud, Gwen)
- Explore the world more—what is the Dragon's Crystal? Why do dragons exist?
- Show Rodney's life leading up to the wedding (his hollowness in scenes with Gwen)
- Follow through on the choice—at least one full scene of consequences
- Add additional characters (King Thomas with depth, Rodney's family who chuckled at Maud's pain)
- This would become Maud's story explicitly, possibly alternating POV

**Scenes I wish I'd written**:
1. The moment Maud touched the crystal and transformed (Rodney's POV, horror and helplessness)
2. The morning Rodney left Maud after the transformation
3. The negotiation where they made their "deal"
4. A scene of Maud alone in the mountains, remembering
5. Rodney with Gwen, going through the motions of courtship while thinking of Maud
6. The actual choice moment—Rodney taking action instead of announcing

**Character moments that got cut**:
- Larry being an actual friend who matters
- Gwen having any personality
- Rodney and Maud's first meeting/courtship
- Maud discovering what she'd become
- King Thomas as a father figure (threatening? understanding? purely political?)
- The squire Simon (mentioned once, could be a mirror/foil)

### Scope Recommendation: **EXPAND**

**Rationale**: 

This story has a powerful thematic core and emotional resonance that is cramped by its current scope. The 1,200-word length forces too much telling instead of showing, particularly for the story's most important moments (the transformation, the abandonment, the depth of their love). 

However, this is **not** a case of "longer is better." The story works at its current length—it's complete and satisfying. But it's working at maybe 70% of its potential power. The transformation scene alone, if shown rather than told, would be devastating. Maud's loneliness, if we felt it, would make her desperation even more heartbreaking. Rodney's cowardice, if we watched him choose the easy path step by step, would make the wedding confrontation more painful.

**Key insight from causality analysis**: The expansion must preserve Rodney's reactive nature in the present timeline. He's not a planner or a hero—he's a man who made cowardly choices and is now being forced to face them. Maud drives the confrontation; Rodney responds. Any added scenes should either:
1. Be flashbacks that show his past cowardice, OR
2. Show him continuing to react in the present, OR
3. Follow the aftermath of his choice

Do NOT add scenes of Rodney planning to do the right thing or becoming proactive before Maud forces his hand. That would break the story's power.

## Phase 3: PATH RECOMMENDATION

### Recommendation: **EXPAND to 3,000-3,500 words**

**Target Scope**: Upper short story / lower novelette  
**Target Structure**: 3-4 scenes with strategic flashbacks

**Rationale**: The story's power lies in its emotional core—Maud's transformation, Rodney's cowardice, the violence of conformity. At 1,200 words, these elements are sketched; at 3,000+ words, they can be felt. This scope allows for showing the transformation scene, deepening character relationships, and following through on the choice without fundamentally restructuring the narrative.

### What to Preserve (The Sacred Core)

1. **The wedding disruption structure** - Opening in medias res at the ceremony stays
2. **Rodney's reactive nature** - He responds to Maud's confrontation; doesn't plan or prepare
3. **Maud as the driver** - She crashes, she confronts, she ultimatums
4. **The thematic core** - Wedding as monster, conformity as violence
5. **Lavender detail** - Perfect sensory memory
6. **Rodney's hollow admission** - "I'd rather let the Kingdom burn"
7. **Maud's humanity in dragon form** - Tears hissing on scales, desperate plea
8. **The subtext-rich dialogue** - Don't over-explain
9. **The decision point** - Though we'll show it with action, not announcement

### What to Add (Major Additions)

1. **Flashback: The Transformation** (~400-500 words)
   - Triggered when Rodney says "I had to watch while you transformed"
   - Show the actual moment: Maud touching the crystal despite his warnings
   - His helplessness, her agony, the horror of watching someone you love become "monstrous"
   - End with him reaching for her, her pulling away
   - Return to present with "And there was nothing I could do"

2. **Flashback: The Morning After** (~300-400 words)
   - Triggered when Maud says "You abandoned me"
   - Show Rodney leaving Maud after her transformation
   - His cowardice rationalized as "giving her space" or "seeking a cure"
   - Her watching him ride away
   - The moment he chose to return to the kingdom and play hero

3. **Gwen's Agency** (~200-300 words in present scene)
   - Gwen doesn't stay unconscious or just cry
   - After Maud's ultimatum, Gwen speaks
   - One or two lines that reveal her as a person (maybe she's known all along? maybe she pities both of them? maybe she's relieved to escape this hollow marriage?)
   - Her words complicate Rodney's choice by removing one of his rationalizations

4. **The Choice Shown** (~200 words)
   - Cut "I've made my decision"
   - Show Rodney's action: pulling the sword from the floorboards, handing it to Gwen or Simon, and walking toward Maud
   - Or: touching Maud's scales and saying "Show me where we're going"
   - Physical action reveals choice

5. **Brief Aftermath** (~300-400 words)
   - Don't end at the decision
   - Show one scene after: Rodney and Maud leaving through the shattered window, flying
   - Or: The congregation breaking free of glamour, the chaos that ensues
   - Just enough to show the first consequence of his choice

### What to Expand (Elements to Develop)

1. **Larry's death** - If keeping it, give him one meaningful exchange before Maud arrives so his death matters. Otherwise cut it entirely.

2. **Maud's present-scene emotional arc** - More interiority from Rodney about what he sees in her: the hurt, the desperation, the humanity beneath the scales.

3. **The glamoured congregation** - Show them under the spell (vacant eyes? forced smiles?) then breaking free when Maud smashes the window or roars.

4. **Rodney's internal monologue** - Richer self-awareness of his cowardice, his hollow pursuit of convention, his genuine conflict (even though we know which way he'll choose).

5. **Physical setting** - More details of the church, the wedding finery, the contrast between beautiful ceremony and blood/destruction.

### What to Cut/Tighten

1. **Opening line** - Remove "She's a smoke show" (or move it to much later as internal Rodney humor after tension is established)
2. **Larry's exposition** - "My best man and best friend Larry, Sir Lawrence to most" → "Larry hung several feet higher"
3. **Repetitive accusations** - Consolidate Maud's three betrayal accusations into one sharper moment
4. **Dragon's Crystal explanation** - Either cut it or make it significant to present action
5. **Explanatory dialogue** - Trust subtext; cut 200-300 words of over-explanation

### Proposed Structure (3,000-3,500 words)

**Scene 1: Wedding Disruption - Present** (~600 words)
- Wedding ceremony, "speak now or forever hold your peace"
- Maud crashes (remove or minimize Larry death for now)
- Initial confrontation begins
- Transition to flashback when transformation is mentioned

**Scene 2: Flashback - The Transformation** (~400 words)
- Maud touches the Dragon's Crystal
- Rodney watches her transform
- His horror, her agony
- The moment their love becomes "impossible"
- Return to present

**Scene 3: Wedding Disruption - Present Continued** (~700 words)
- Confrontation escalates
- Maud reveals the deal they made
- Transition to second flashback

**Scene 4: Flashback - The Abandonment** (~300 words)
- Rodney leaves Maud the morning after transformation
- His cowardice masked as nobility
- Returns to kingdom to play hero
- Return to present

**Scene 5: Wedding Disruption - Ultimatum & Choice** (~800 words)
- Maud's ultimatum: roast everyone or you come with me
- Gwen speaks (complicates the choice)
- Rodney's internal conflict deepens
- The glamour breaks (congregation reacts)
- Rodney makes his choice through action, not announcement
- Brief aftermath (~200 words): first consequence of choice

**Total: ~3,200 words**
