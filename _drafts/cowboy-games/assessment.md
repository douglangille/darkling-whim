# Assessment: Cowboy Games

**Original Publication**: March 20, 2014  
**Current Word Count**: 602 words  
**Assessment Date**: December 29, 2025

---

## Initial Analysis

### Core Elements

**Premise**: Two lonely elderly residents at a care home find human connection and intimacy through roleplay on a night when one contemplates suicide.

**Promise**: The title and opening promise a story about the forgotten elderly in institutional care, with hints of both darkness ("dead-end street," "dying town") and unexpected life within.

**Payoff**: Yes - the story delivers a bittersweet twist. What begins as contemplation of suicide transforms into an unexpected moment of human connection, intimacy, and survival. The "cowboy games" aren't childish but deeply human.

**Heart**: Loneliness can be both fatal and healing. Connection - even unconventional connection - saves lives.

**Theme**: Isolation, abandonment by society/family, the continued need for human intimacy and dignity in old age, choosing life over death.

### Causality & Character Agency

**Character Agency**: Mixed - reactive turning proactive

**Key Discoveries**: Accidental - the timing is serendipitous

**Plot Driver**: Circumstances create opportunity; character choices determine outcome

**Expansion Considerations**: **CRITICAL - The accidental nature of the interruption is core to the story's power.** Jameson's knock happens at the exact moment Eileen is considering suicide. This cannot become a planned "wellness check" or deliberate intervention. The story's heart depends on two lonely souls accidentally finding each other. Any expansion must preserve this serendipity. If Jameson becomes a character who "notices" Eileen's distress, the magic dies.

### What Works

1. **The Setup**: The opening paragraph establishes tone perfectly - arrivals vs departures, weeds vs visitors, the poetry of decay
2. **The Reversal**: Excellent misdirection - we expect tragedy, get human connection instead
3. **The Ending**: The final line is perfect - suggestive without being explicit, poignant without being maudlin
4. **Compression**: Every detail serves the story - no wasted words
5. **Voice**: Matter-of-fact narration creates emotional distance that makes the intimacy hit harder

### What Needs Work

1. **Mister Jameson's Character**: We get vivid detail about Eileen (40 years teaching, son David, brandy, charm) but Jameson is mostly mystery - "longer tenure than everyone" but no concrete past. The syphilis detail feels gossipy/unreliable.
2. **The Seduction**: The transition from "interrupted suicide" to "intimacy" happens in two sentences. We get logistics (door opening, robe falling) but not emotional bridge.
3. **Timing Questions**: Why this night? What made tonight the night Eileen reached breaking point? The brandy suggests premeditation but feels unexplored.

### Surprises on Re-reading

- The "peddle her charms" line in paragraph 5 establishes Eileen's agency and sexuality, making the ending feel more earned
- "Marshall Dillon" reference is perfect for the generation - they'd remember Gunsmoke (1955-1975)
- The story never explicitly states what happens, but the final image is unmistakable
- The title works on multiple levels - games as roleplay, as distraction, as survival mechanism

## Scope Analysis

**Current Scope**: 602 words, 3 scenes (Eileen alone → Jameson knocks → Eileen opens door), spans approximately 30-60 minutes of story time

**Pacing Assessment**: **Appropriate for flash** - moves quickly but earned. The compression serves the emotional punch.

### Expansion Candidates

1. **Jameson's Backstory**: "His tenure longer than everyone else" - what's his story?
2. **The Relationship**: This reads like it might not be the first time. Has this happened before?
3. **David's Story**: Son who abandoned her - what's his life like? Powerful counterpoint.
4. **The Morning After**: What happens next? Does this change things?
5. **Other Residents**: "The word on the ward" - there's a community here.
6. **Eileen's Teaching Past**: 40 years of shaping young lives, now forgotten.
7. **The Suicide Planning**: The wall sconce, the bathrobe tie - thought through before tonight?

### Scenes I Wish I'd Written

1. Jameson's preparation - putting on the boots and hat, practicing the drawl, working up courage to knock
2. The morning after - awkward? comfortable? transformative?
3. A flashback to Eileen's last good day with David before he stopped visiting
4. Another resident's perspective on the two of them

### Character Moments That Got Cut

- What Eileen was like as a teacher (we know duration but not quality)
- Jameson's actual past vs. the syphilis gossip
- The moment Eileen realized David wasn't coming back
- What Jameson sees when Eileen opens the door that makes him stay

## Path Recommendation

### Recommendation: **EXPAND** (minimally to 800-900 words)

**Rationale**: The core story is excellent but the transition from suicide to intimacy happens too fast (four paragraphs). Adding character depth and an emotional bridge will strengthen the story while preserving its flash fiction compression and power.

### Specific Next Steps

**Target Scope**: 800-900 words (staying in flash fiction)

**PRESERVE** (DO NOT CHANGE):
- ✅ The accidental timing of Jameson's knock
- ✅ The opening paragraph (perfect as-is)
- ✅ The final line/image (perfect as-is)
- ✅ The compression of time
- ✅ The ambiguity about what exactly happens
- ✅ Eileen's agency in opening the door
- ✅ The tone - matter-of-fact narration with emotional undercurrent
- ✅ "The wall sconce would be there tomorrow" (thesis statement)

**ADD**:
1. **Jameson's Scene Before** (50-100 words): Show him in his room before he wanders. Why is HE awake? The cowboy outfit routine. **Critical: Don't make him aware of Eileen's crisis.**

2. **Emotional Bridge at Door** (50-75 words): Between the door opening and the robe falling - Eileen's interior moment, the decision point. Hand on knob, weighing the choice between the sconce and whatever's on the other side.

3. **Brief Backstory Reveals** (100-125 words total, woven in):
   - Eileen: One specific teaching memory that shows who she was
   - Eileen: The moment she realized David was gone
   - Jameson: One detail that complicates the syphilis gossip

**EXPAND**:
1. The suicide contemplation - make it more visceral, more real
2. The "peddle her charms" moment - establish Eileen's personality
3. Jameson's character beyond the costume

## Previous Assessment Notes (External)

From prior critical review:

- "The wall sconce would be there tomorrow" is the story's thesis in nine words
- The story operates on misdirection—setup suicide attempt, pivot to life-affirming connection
- Clean three-act structure works
- The final paragraph's reveal (pistol = erection, housecoat drops) reframes as consensual sexual connection
- Story argues human connection—even transgressive—trumps despair
- Takes real risks with subject matter and earns them through full humanity

**Key suggestion**: Add one beat where Eileen hesitates at the door—hand on knob, weighing the choice between the sconce and whatever's on the other side. This makes it clear she's choosing connection over death, not just being distracted from it.

Suggested text:
> She stood at the door, hand on the knob. Behind her, the sconce. Ahead, god knows what. She thought of David's last visit, how he'd looked past her the whole time. Tomorrow the sconce would still be there. She opened the door.

This makes her agency explicit and the pivot feel earned rather than convenient.
