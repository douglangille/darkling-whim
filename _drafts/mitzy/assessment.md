# Story Assessment: Mitzy and the Butterfly

**Date**: December 23, 2025  
**Original Publication**: August 21, 2015 (douglangille.ca)  
**Current Scope**: Flash Fiction / Dark Vignette  
**Current Word Count**: ~1,200 words

---

## Story Summary

Young Mitzy wakes in an overgrown boxwood maze after her little sister June has vanished through "white magic." Guided by a sarcastic talking butterfly named Larry, Mitzy navigates the maze with an iron spear to kill the Goblin and rescue June. At the appointed hour, she finds her target and thrusts her weapon—only to discover she has killed her own sister, completing a tragic circle of family resentment and magical manipulation.

---

## Current State Analysis

### What's Working (Preserve These)

- **The circular frame**: Opening and closing with "White magic. My little sister. Gone" creates devastating symmetry
- **The voice**: Immediate, intimate third-person with excellent internal dialogue italics that reveal character through thought
- **The ambiguity**: June/Fake-June duality reveals family tension without exposition
- **The twist execution**: Genuinely surprising yet retroactively inevitable
- **Larry the Butterfly**: Perfectly calibrated guide character—helpful, sarcastic, morally ambiguous, disappears before the tragedy
- **The horror restraint**: No gore, no exploitation—the tragedy hits emotionally, not viscerally
- **The fairy tale logic**: Dream-reality blur, talking animals, magical mazes—all feel right for the genre
- **Sensory detail**: Heat, humidity, breathing, the iron post—grounded despite unreality
- **The spear moment**: "Iron Ivy" naming and the physicality of pulling it free
- **Thematic complexity**: Resentment, family dysfunction, self-fulfilling prophecy

### What's Compressed (Could Expand)

- **Before-the-maze**: How did Mitzy and June arrive here? What's their relationship history?
- **The family dysfunction**: Why is June "Fake June"? Adoption? Replacement? Favoritism?
- **The Goblin mythology**: What is it? Where did it come from? Cultural context?
- **Larry's nature**: What/who is he really? Guide, trickster, demon, test?
- **The maze itself**: Who built it? What's its purpose? Is it real or mental/magical?
- **The hour countdown**: Time warps in the maze but the deadline matters—why?
- **Mitzy's character**: Age, personality beyond resentment, skills, fears
- **June's agency**: Does she have any? Is she conscious? Fighting back?
- **The moment of arrival**: "Her bed, back home, felt real"—what's the transition?
- **Aftermath**: The moment stops at revelation—what happens next?

### What's Missing (If Expanded)

- **Stakes establishment**: What does "gone" mean before the maze? Is June dead already?
- **World-building**: Is this Earth? Fantasy realm? Dream logic only?
- **Supporting characters**: Parents? The Uncle Bart mentioned? Who else cares about June?
- **Mitzy's internal conflict**: The story shows resentment but not the struggle against it
- **The magic system**: How does "white magic" work? Who wields it?
- **Alternative paths**: In a longer version, what other choices could Mitzy make?
- **Sensory richness**: More maze sections, more environments, more texture
- **June's perspective**: Even brief glimpses could deepen the tragedy
- **The resolution**: Does Mitzy escape? Learn? Change? Or is this hell?

## Scope Analysis

### Current Scope
**Flash Fiction / Dark Vignette** - ~1,200 words

Functions as: A complete tragic fairy tale in miniature. The twist landing requires compression—if readers had more time with the story, the revelation would need more setup. Works because it hits fast and hard.

### Natural Scope
**Short Story** - 4,000-6,000 words

The material suggests: This wants to be a full short story with proper three-act structure. The current version reads like a brilliant Act III that's missing Acts I and II. There's a complete character arc (resentment → action → recognition → tragedy), but we only see the final movement. The emotional impact would deepen with proper setup.

Alternative consideration: **Novella** - 15,000-20,000 words if treating as YA dark fantasy with multiple POVs and full world-building.

### Expansion Potential: **HIGH**

**Reasoning**: 
- The compression is intentional and effective for flash, but there's clearly MORE story here
- Every element mentioned begs questions that could be answered without breaking the fairy tale logic
- The emotional core is strong enough to support more weight
- The twist works at flash length but would work BETTER with earned emotional investment
- Multiple clear expansion paths that don't dilute the impact
- The world-building hints suggest a richer mythology
- Character relationships are implied but not explored
- The author's voice can absolutely sustain longer form—the prose is confident and controlled

## Path Recommendations

### ⭐ RECOMMENDED: Option B - EXPANSION to Short Story

**Path**: Expand to 4,000-6,000 word complete short story

**Why**: 

1. **The material is there**: Everything needed is implied in the current version. Expansion means fulfillment, not invention.

2. **Emotional deepening**: The twist works now, but with proper setup it could be devastating. Earning the reader's investment in both sisters would make the tragedy land harder.

3. **Character development**: Mitzy currently functions as an archetype. A short story allows her to become a full person with contradictions, growth, and agency.

4. **The voice can handle it**: Your prose is controlled and confident. The style won't buckle under additional weight—it'll flourish with more room.

5. **Market viability**: 5,000-word short stories have better markets than flash. More venues, better pay, anthology potential.

6. **Author development**: This is excellent practice for sustained narrative. The bones are strong; building muscle on them serves your craft.

7. **Reader satisfaction**: The current version impresses but leaves readers wanting more (good wanting, not frustrated wanting). A short story would satisfy while maintaining impact.

8. **Preservation of strengths**: All current strengths can be maintained. The twist, the voice, the fairy tale logic, the restraint—none of these require dilution.

**Work Involved**:
- Draft 4-5 new scenes (3,000 words)
- Revise existing material for integration
- 2-3 full revision passes
- Line editing pass
- Final polish

**Expected Outcome**: 
A complete 5,000-word short story that maintains the flash version's impact while delivering fuller emotional and narrative satisfaction. Suitable for submission to pro markets, inclusion in a collection, or standalone publication.

## Expansion Roadmap (if chosen)

### Additional Scenes Needed for Short Story (4,000-6,000 words)

**Scene 1: THE BEFORE** (~1,000 words)
- Mitzy and June at home before the maze
- Establish their relationship: why "Fake June"?
- Show the inciting incident: How does June vanish?
- Mitzy's choice to follow into the maze
- Reveal character: Mitzy's age, personality, skills

**Scene 2: THE ENTRANCE** (~300 words)
- Transition from real world to maze
- First encounter with Larry
- Discovering the sign and making the deal
- Acquiring the Iron Ivy spear
- Decision point: go forward or turn back

**Scene 3: THE TRIALS** (~800 words)
- Multiple maze sections/chambers
- Encounters that test Mitzy's resolve
- Moments where she questions her resentment
- False paths, near-misses, growing desperation
- Larry's guidance becoming more ambiguous

**Scene 4: THE CHOICE** (~500 words)
- Penultimate moment before the final turn
- Opportunity to turn back or see clearly
- Internal struggle: does she want to save June or prove she's right?
- Larry's departure more loaded with meaning

**Scene 5: THE AFTERMATH** (~400 words)
- Immediate aftermath of killing June
- Realization of manipulation
- The maze's true purpose revealed
- Mitzy's fate/transformation/lesson

### Character Development Opportunities

**Mitzy**: From resentful older sister to someone who must confront her own darkness. Show her love for June fighting with resentment. Give her agency and choice rather than just following Larry.

**June**: Even if absent, give her presence through memories, echoes, or appearances. Make her real enough that her loss matters viscerally.

**Larry**: Develop his mythology. Is he a psychopomp? A trickster? A manifestation of Mitzy's own dark thoughts?

### Thematic Deepening

- Resentment vs. love in families
- Self-fulfilling prophecies
- The danger of wishes/getting what you think you want
- Childhood cruelty and its consequences
- Grief, replacement, and what makes family real

## Assessment Notes

This is strong work that punches well above its weight class. The twist is genuinely good—it doesn't feel like a trick, it feels like tragedy. The family dynamics are complex without being explained to death. Larry is a memorable character in just a few lines.

The compression serves the flash format, but this reads like a longer story that's been compressed, not like a story that's naturally flash-length. There's a richness in the margins that wants to be explored.

Your voice is confident enough for the expansion. The real question is: do you want to spend time deepening this world, or move on to other work? Both are valid choices.

If you expand, you have the opportunity to create something that could find wider publication. If you keep it as-is, you have a sharp, effective piece that does what it sets out to do.
